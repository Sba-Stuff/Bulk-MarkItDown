What is Harassment?

Harassment is any improper and unwelcome
conduct that might reasonably be expected
or be perceived to cause offence or
humiliation to another person.

(UN Women)

Examples:

● Making inappropriate sexual jokes
● Commenting derogatorily on someone's race or gender
● Excluding someone based on a disability
● Using verbal insults or offensive language
● Spreading rumors about a colleague
● Intimidating or threatening behavior by a colleague or

supervisor

Forms Of Harassment At Workplace

➔ Discriminatory Harassment: Unfair treatment based on

protected characteristics like age, gender, race, religion, or
sexual orientation.

➔ Personal Harassment: Repeated actions meant to

intimidate or humiliate, based on personal conflicts rather
than protected characteristics.

➔ Physical Harassment: Harmful physical acts such as
unwanted touching, threats, or damage to property.

➔ Sexual Harassment: Unwelcome sexual advances, jokes,
gestures, or any sexual conduct that creates a hostile
environment.

➔ Power Harassment: Abuse of authority to intimidate or pressure
subordinates, such as unrealistic demands or bullying using
hierarchical power.

➔ Psychological Harassment: Mental abuse through humiliation,
isolation, belittling, or discrediting, impacting mental health.

➔ Retaliation Harassment: Mistreatment as revenge for reporting

misconduct or harassment.

➔ Quid Pro Quo Harassment: Demanding favors in exchange for
professional benefits or threatening negative consequences.

➔ Online Harassment (Cyberbullying): Threats, insults, or demeaning

comments via digital platforms like email, chat, or social media.

➔ Verbal Harassment: Hostile or abusive language including insults,

slurs, offensive jokes, persistent criticism, and gossiping.

What is Discrimination?

Discrimination is any unfair treatment or
arbitrary distinction based on a person’s
race, sex, religion, nationality, ethnic origin,
sexual orientation, disability, age, language,
social origin or other status.

(UN Women)

Examples:

● A person is not hired or paid less because of their race or

gender.

● An older employee is excluded from training opportunities in

favor of younger colleagues.

● An employee is denied a promotion because she is

pregnant.

Forms Of Discrimination At Workplace

➔ Race and Color Discrimination: Treating someone
unfavorably because of their race or skin color.

➔ Disability Discrimination: Unfair treatment of employees

with physical or mental disabilities’

➔ Gender Discrimination: Treating employees differently

based on their gender, including pregnancy discrimination.

➔ Age Discrimination: Discriminating

against employees based on their age,
often targeting older workers.

➔ Religion Discrimination: Discrimination
based on religious beliefs or practices.

➔ National Origin Discrimination: Treating an employee

unfavorably because of their birthplace, ethnicity, or accent.

➔ Marital or Family Status Discrimination: Discrimination
based on marital status or responsibilities towards family.

➔ Indirect Discrimination: Neutral policies or practices that

disproportionately disadvantage certain groups without valid
justification.

Federal Ombudsperson Secretariat for
Protection Against Harassment (FOSPAH)

● Autonomous quasi-judicial statutory body established under the
Protection Against Harassment of Women at the Workplace
Act, 2010.

● Primary mandate to address and prevent workplace

harassment, ensuring safe and respectful work environments.
● Authorized to receive, investigate, and adjudicate complaints of

harassment at workplaces nationwide.

● Has the power to recommend disciplinary action, impose
penalties, and award compensation in harassment cases.

● Ensures all organizations establish internal complaint

committees to handle harassment complaints.

● Conducts awareness campaigns and educates the
public about rights and responsibilities related to
workplace harassment and women's property rights.
● Operates regional offices for accessibility and timely

complaint resolution.

● Provides speedy justice and promotes accountability and

dignity for women at workplaces.

● Led by the Federal Ombudsperson who oversees

organizational functions and enforces compliance with laws.

● Committed to protecting individuals of all genders from

harassment and discrimination.

● Acts as a critical institution in Pakistan’s governance system
to safeguard women’s economic empowerment and gender
equality.

Uzma Naveed Chaudhry v. Federation of Pakistan (PLD 2022 SC 783)

● Uzma Naveed Chaudhry filed a complaint of sexual harassment and hostile

behavior against several colleagues at Pakistan Television.

● She faced verbal abuse, sexist remarks, and a generally hostile work

environment that affected her mental well-being.

● Despite reporting the incidents, the organization's management failed to

effectively address or investigate the harassment.

● The Supreme Court upheld her complaint, reinforcing protections against

workplace harassment under Pakistani law, even after resignation.
● The case highlights the challenges women face in hostile workplace

environments and the legal recourse available against discrimination and
harassment.

International Labour Organization (ILO)

Convention No. 111 (C111)

● ILO Convention No. 111 (C111) is the International Labour

Organization’s Discrimination (Employment and Occupation)
Convention, adopted in 1958.

● It aims to eliminate discrimination in employment and occupation.
● Defines discrimination as any distinction, exclusion, or preference
made based on race, color, sex, religion, political opinion, national
extraction, or social origin that nullifies or impairs equality of
opportunity or treatment in employment.

● It requires ratifying states to adopt laws and policies to prohibit

discrimination and promote equality of opportunity and treatment in
employment and occupation.

● Applies broadly to all workers in all sectors, including public, private,

formal, and informal economies.

● Grounds for discrimination can be expanded by states through

consultation with employers and workers.

● The Convention is one of the ILO’s fundamental conventions and is

widely ratified by member states.

● It is complemented by Recommendation No. 111, which provides

non-binding guidelines for application.

International Labour Organization (ILO)

Convention No. 190 (C190)

● First international treaty to explicitly address violence and
harassment in the world of work, including gender-based
violence.

● Defines violence and harassment as unacceptable behaviors

or threats causing physical, psychological, sexual, or
economic harm, including single or repeated occurrences.

● Protects all workers, including employees, interns,

apprentices, volunteers, job seekers, and those with
terminated contracts.

● Extends protection beyond the traditional workplace to cover

work-related environments such as private workspaces, break rooms,
employer-provided accommodation, travel for work, work events, work
communications (including digital), and commuting.

● Mandates national laws to prohibit violence and harassment and

adopt prevention, protection, and complaint mechanisms.

● Requires mechanisms ensuring privacy, protection from retaliation,
and support (legal, social, medical, administrative) for complainants
and victims.

● Employees have the right to remove themselves from

dangerous work situations without penalty.

● Recognizes the evolving nature of work, including digital forms

of work and non-standard employment arrangements.
● Supports gender-responsive approaches and policies to

address gender-based violence at work.

● Calls for effective sanctions against perpetrators and promotes

a culture of respect and dignity in the workplace.

Behavior Guidelines in the Workplace

Behavior Guidelines for Employees

a. Maintain Professional Conduct

● Treat everyone with dignity and respect, regardless of gender, rank, culture, or

background.

● Use polite, inclusive, and gender-neutral language.

● Avoid offensive jokes, gossip, or comments about someone’s appearance or personal

life.

● Keep professional boundaries — avoid unnecessary physical contact or overly personal

conversations.

Behavior Guidelines for Employees

b. Communication Etiquette

● Be mindful of tone (in person and online).

● Refrain from using language, emojis, or humour that could be

misinterpreted.

● Respect others’ privacy and personal space.

Behavior Guidelines for Employees

c. Accountability

● Take responsibility for your actions and language.

● If someone expresses discomfort, listen and adjust your behavior

immediately.

● Don’t trivialize harassment or discrimination complaints — show empathy

and understanding.

Behavior Guidelines for Managers

a. Lead by Example

● Model respectful, inclusive, and fair behavior at all times.

● Be conscious of the power dynamic — avoid actions that could be seen as

coercive or intimidating.

● Maintain professionalism in both formal and informal settings (including office

events or online chats).

Behavior Guidelines for Managers

b. Foster an Inclusive Environment

● Encourage open communication and feedback.

● Ensure all employees have equal access to opportunities, mentorship, and

participation.

● Recognize and address biases in hiring, promotions, or task assignments.

Behavior Guidelines for Managers

c. Respond to Complaints Appropriately

● Take all complaints seriously, no matter how small they seem.

● Maintain confidentiality while investigating.

● Avoid retaliation or favoritism.

● Ensure the reporting process is transparent and fair.

Role of the Organization

● Develop clear policies on harassment and discrimination with behavior

expectations.

● Provide regular training and communication on respectful workplace

behavior.

● Establish conﬁdential reporting and complaint resolution mechanisms.
● Foster a culture that values diversity, inclusivity, and respect.
● Monitor and evaluate workplace climate and take corrective actions as

needed.

● Protect employees from retaliation after reporting misconduct.

Role of Bystanders

● Recognize signs of harassment or discrimination.
● Intervene safely or support the affected individual

when possible.

● Report observed incidents to management or HR.
● Encourage a respectful workplace culture

collectively.
